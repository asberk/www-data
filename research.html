Signal and image processing

Researchers — in industry and academia alike — rely on the ability to manipulate empirically-acquired image data to give meaningful interpretation to experimental and numerical results. Further, a market exists in which consumers must send and receive information at very fast rates and with imperceptible loss in quality. With ever-increasing data sizes, and ever-improving methods of data acquisition, efficacious methods for data storage, retrieval, communication, processing, analysis and representation are imperative.

Most recently, I have worked in problems related to the perceptual representation of data — from stratifying the levels of detail in an image/signal to de-noising, de-convolution, segmentation, classification and in-painting. Connections with these problems have been shown in terms of Learning problems, and accurately classifying and predicting outcomes using large datasets. Examples of the above are given in my M.Sc. project document.

Geophysical problems

Accurately determining and predicting climate change and weather patterns is of utmost importance for sustaining economic growth, wildlife diversity and human life. For example, the devastation caused by tsunamis is of constant concern to coastal regions around the world. Results from tidal flow simulations are useful in determining patterns of wave flow and dispersion, having applications to barrier and structural design for the prevention of damage caused by tidal events. Moreover, tools developed in climate modelling have important applications in other areas of mathematics, in economics, and computer vision.

I have worked on problems directly related to realistic simulations of tidal flow. Due to the immensity of scales that persist in tidal flow, it is important to develop a means by which to accurately represent all scales while minimizing resultant computational complexity. In spite of this, simulations are often still extremely computationally demanding. Wavelets and level set methods are two important examples of the kinds of tools used in geophysical applications.

Mathematical biology

Important for human health — whether at the population level, concerned with the spread of disease between human beings or among children; or at the level of the cell, concerned with cell growth and tissue morphology.

I have worked on problems relating to the metastatic potential of tumour cell cohorts when subject to a chemotactic enzyme gradient, having suggested that tumour cell cohorts are more likely to metastasize when there exists an apparent enzyme gradient to which the cancerous cells are attracted.

Additionally, I have worked on problems relating to epidemiology and population ecology. I have used SHARCNET to optimize statistical models of plant distributions by using lare-scale sparse datasets of heterskedastic seed and seedling distribution to fit spatial auto-correlation functions to the dispersal patterns. In addition I have used stochastic and deterministic techniques to simulate a spatial SIR model of human disease epidemics.
